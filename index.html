Section 01 — Executive Summary

A complete capital plan for AEGIS — from incorporation to self-sustaining commercial operation.

This Detailed Project Report defines the engineering, infrastructure, organisational, regulatory, and financial scope required to deliver the AEGIS NOC/SOC platform from inception through the cash-flow-positive inflection point — a 36-month programme structured around five sequential phases with explicit gating and a total outlay of Rs 13.50 crore.

Capital thesis

The AEGIS NOC/SOC platform is a sovereign-deployed, AI-native security operations platform engineered for Indian regulated industries — banking, financial services, insurance, capital markets, payments — where the combination of regulatory weight, data-residency requirements, and adversary sophistication has rendered hyperscaler-cloud-native security platforms structurally inadequate. This Detailed Project Report defines the engineering, infrastructure, organisational, regulatory, and financial scope required to deliver the platform from inception through the cash-flow-positive inflection point — a single thirty-six-month capital deployment from incorporation through to the moment at which monthly customer revenue exceeds monthly burn.

The reason this document scopes the entire thirty-six-month arc rather than a narrower build-phase window is that the most operationally expensive period of the programme is not the build itself but the fourteen-month interval between general availability and revenue self-sufficiency. A capital plan that ends at general availability ignores the period during which the company must continue to fund a twenty-two-person team while customer revenue ramps from a handful of pilot conversions to a self-sustaining annual run-rate. The correct unit of analysis for an institutional capital plan is the full arc to the commercial inflection point.

The total outlay is Rs 13.50 crore, decomposed across four cost categories. The build cost from month zero through enterprise-grade general availability is Rs 5.40 crore — Rs 0.20 crore for foundation and validation, Rs 0.50 crore for the core minimum viable product, Rs 0.85 crore for the pilot-ready product release, and Rs 3.85 crore for the enterprise-grade productionisation programme during which all six AEGIS machine-learning models reach production status and all six AlgoForge product modules reach general availability. The post-general-availability operations runway from month twenty-two through month thirty-six is Rs 6.80 crore and represents the largest single category in the programme — compensation continues to dominate the cost stack as the team operates the platform for paying customers, refreshes annual certifications, and develops the commercial pipeline. The transaction and reserve costs aggregate Rs 1.30 crore, comprising Series A round closing fees, ongoing legal and counsel work, and a working-capital buffer sized at five per cent of total outlay against unbudgeted operational events.

The net external capital requirement is materially smaller than the gross outlay. Customer pilot revenue beginning at month eight, ramping through pilot conversion at month fourteen, and accelerating after general availability is conservatively projected to recognise Rs 4.50 crore over the programme window. Subtracting expected revenue from gross outlay yields a net capital requirement of Rs 9.00 crore — structured as approximately Rs 2 crore in seed and founders' capital largely already deployed, plus a Rs 7 crore Series A institutional round targeted to close at month twenty-four against the strength of three reference paid customers and a Rs 3.92 crore base-case annual recurring revenue trajectory.

The financial structure is anchored to operational reality rather than aspiration. Quisitive Businesses LLP holds three concurrently issued ISO management-system certifications dated twenty-seventh of February 2026 — ISO/IEC 27001:2022 information security, ISO 9001:2015 quality, and ISO/IEC 20000-1:2018 IT service management — covering end-to-end NOC services, SOC managed services, and cloud services from the registered facility at the Artha Special Economic Zone in Greater Noida. SOC 2 Type II attestation across all five Trust Services Criteria is in active processing. The Rs 3.85 crore high-performance compute installation that hosts the platform is already deployed: 128 Xeon Sapphire Rapids cores, 1.4 TB of DDR5-ECC memory, 23 TB of SAN-class solid-state storage, a FortiGate 120G high-availability pair, and four NVIDIA A100 80 GB graphical processing units. The platform is not being designed against an imagined infrastructure; it is being engineered against measured infrastructure already racked, commissioned, and producing telemetry.

The capital-plan distinction

A build-cost number tells you what it costs to make the product. A capital-plan number tells you what it costs to survive long enough for the product to make money. The Rs 5.40 crore build cost is necessary but not sufficient — without the additional Rs 8.10 crore that funds team operation through customer revenue ramp, certifications refresh, sales pipeline development, and the working-capital buffer that absorbs unbudgeted operational events, the build investment never reaches its commercial inflection. The Rs 13.50 crore figure represents the honest full-arc capital plan from incorporation to commercial self-sufficiency.

Section 03 — Project Rationale

Why a 36-month full-cycle programme rather than a v1-only build.

A capital plan that ends at general availability assumes that the product, once built, will generate revenue fast enough to fund ongoing operations from its first month of commercial sale. That assumption is incorrect for Indian BFSI cybersecurity and dangerous to make uncritically.

The case for full-cycle funding

Indian banking, financial services, and insurance procurement cycles span six to twelve months even for signed pilot relationships, and twelve to twenty-four months for tier-one bank deployments. A platform that reaches general availability at month twenty-two will not see meaningful annual recurring revenue accretion until month twenty-six at earliest, with conservative scenarios placing material ARR scale at month thirty-two or beyond. The fourteen months between general availability and revenue self-sufficiency are the most operationally expensive period of the programme — twenty-two-plus FTE on payroll, infrastructure expanding to support paying customers, annual certification cycles refreshing, sales investment ramping to develop pipeline. A programme funded only through general availability runs out of capital precisely when commercial validation begins to arrive, forcing emergency bridge financing on terms that are materially worse than capital raised on schedule against a planned milestone.

The alternative — funding the full thirty-six-month cycle in advance — produces three structural advantages. First, it removes the capital-shortage risk from the operational team's attention budget; engineers and sales leaders focus on the work, not on the next round. Second, it allows the Series A round to close on the schedule that is commercially optimal (against three reference customers and demonstrated annual recurring revenue trajectory) rather than the schedule that is forced by cash exhaustion. Third, it forces honest scoping of the operations period — the second-largest cost bucket in the programme — into the formal capital plan rather than treating it as a problem for future fundraising.

The financial logic of full-cycle funding is straightforward: the difference between the build cost (Rs 5.40 crore) and the full programme outlay (Rs 13.50 crore) is Rs 8.10 crore, of which Rs 4.50 crore is offset by expected customer revenue over the same window. The genuine incremental capital ask is Rs 3.60 crore — the cost of buying fourteen months of operating runway through to cash-flow break-even rather than gambling on revenue arriving before capital exhausts. That incremental Rs 3.60 crore is the single best risk-management investment in the programme.

Quisitive-specific advantages

The Rs 13.50 crore total is materially smaller than what an equivalent green-field competitor would face. The Rs 3.85 crore Greater Noida high-performance compute installation is already deployed and amortising — a green-field competitor would carry an additional Rs 6.5–8 crore of infrastructure capex. The three ISO certifications dated February 2026 reduce the SOC 2 Type II audit timeline by approximately four months and the audit fee by approximately fifteen per cent. The shared LLP back-office (finance, HR, compliance, legal counsel) cost-allocates across three verticals rather than burdening cybersecurity alone. Including these advantages, an equivalent green-field cybersecurity startup pursuing the same scope would face a programme outlay of approximately Rs 22–25 crore — Quisitive's structural cost advantage is between Rs 8 and Rs 11 crore against new entrants.

Per-customer isolation architecture

The platform's defence-in-depth tenant-isolation architecture spans six control layers. Cross-tenant data exposure due to misconfiguration or implementation defect is the single most consequential risk in a multi-tenant security platform; the architectural commitment is that no single-layer failure can produce a cross-tenant breach.

  1. Network plane. Per-customer Kubernetes namespace with NetworkPolicy enforcement. Egress restricted to customer-specific allow-list. East-west traffic between customer pods is denied by default; explicit allow rules required per integration.
  2. Compute plane. Pod-level resource quotas. Per-customer service accounts with least-privilege RBAC. No shared workload across tenants — even shared-infrastructure components (Kafka, OpenSearch) operate under per-tenant authentication contexts.
  3. Storage plane. Per-customer storage volumes with separate encryption keys. Object-store buckets per customer with bucket-policy denying cross-account access. WORM-bucket configuration for audit log immutability.
  4. Identity plane. Per-customer Keycloak realm with realm-isolation enforcement. No cross-realm trust. Per-customer SAML/OIDC integrations with customer's own identity provider.
  5. Application plane. Tenant context propagation through all microservice calls. Tenant identifier cryptographically bound to request via JWT claim. Cross-tenant query attempts logged and alerted.
  6. Audit plane. Per-customer audit log streams to immutable WORM storage. Regulator-readable export pipeline operates per-customer with no implicit cross-customer access.

Cross-tenant exposure remains the existential operational risk; the six-layer architecture reduces it but does not eliminate it. Defence-in-depth is augmented by an isolation regression suite of two hundred-plus test cases that gates every release, an external penetration test specifically targeting cross-tenant exposure, and cyber-liability insurance scaled to Rs 50 crore from Phase 3 onwards.

Series A round structuring

Round structure

The Series A round is sized at Rs 7 crore against an Rs 80–120 crore post-money valuation in base-case scenario. The valuation envelope is calibrated to comparable Indian deep-tech cybersecurity Series A rounds in 2024–2025 — Safe Security's USD 70 million Series C in 2024 led by Cisco Investments anchors the upper end of the comparable set, while Druva's earlier rounds at smaller valuations anchor the lower end. Quisitive's combination of three reference customers, Rs 1.5 Cr+ ARR run-rate, BFSI-native compliance posture, and existing infrastructure deployment positions the round in the middle of this comparable set.

Target lead investors are Indian deep-tech-aware funds with cybersecurity portfolio depth — Peak XV (formerly Sequoia India), Elevation Capital, Lightspeed India, and Blume Ventures form the primary outreach set. Strategic-investor participation from Cisco Investments, Mastercard Foundation, or analogous BFSI-aligned strategics is sought as a secondary co-investor layer. The investment-bank advisory is calibrated to round size — a boutique adviser at Rs 7 crore round size is the appropriate engagement structure rather than a bulge-bracket bank.

Round closing fees are budgeted at Rs 55 lakh — approximately seven per cent of round size at the upper end of the typical four-to-seven-per-cent range for Indian Series A rounds. This includes investment-bank fees (three per cent), legal counsel (one and a half per cent), due-diligence response cost in the form of internal team time and external specialist support (one per cent), and miscellaneous transaction costs (half per cent).

Bridge instruments and non-dilutive options

  • Startup India Seed Fund Scheme. Up to Rs 20 lakh non-dilutive grant available for proof-of-concept validation; suitable for Phase 0 / Phase 1 bridge if Series-Seed conversations slip.
  • BIRAC Biotechnology Industry Partnership Programme. Cost-sharing instrument adjacent to the bioinformatics vertical that does not directly fund cybersecurity but improves the LLP-level cash position by offsetting bioinformatics-vertical opex.
  • RBI HaRBInger initiative. Regulatory sandbox engagement provides credibility-amplification rather than direct funding; a successful HaRBInger engagement materially improves Series A valuation.
  • Convertible debt bridge (if needed). If Series A round closing slips beyond M24, a convertible-debt bridge from existing seed investors or strategic partners is the preferred bridge instrument over emergency equity dilution. Discount to next round 15–25%; valuation cap aligned to internal Series A target.
Why the Series A is sized at Rs 7 Cr rather than the larger round common in cybersecurity

A Series A of Rs 12–15 Cr would be readily defensible given the team, infrastructure, and customer trajectory at M24. The deliberate decision to size at Rs 7 Cr is a dilution-management choice. Rs 7 Cr is the minimum capital required to bridge from M24 to cash-flow break-even at M32–M34 with appropriate working-capital buffer. Any capital beyond that floor is taken at Series A valuation rather than at a higher Series B valuation twelve months later. The dilution arithmetic favours a tight Series A and a stronger Series B, provided the company has confidence in its commercial trajectory — which the three-customer reference base and Rs 1.5 Cr ARR run-rate at M24 supports.

Section 16 — Risk Register

Programme-fatal risks with named owners and quantified mitigations.

A programme-level risk register is structurally different from a phase-level one — it surfaces risks that span phases, that interact, or that become severe only when compounding effects accumulate. The registry below covers risks specific to the full thirty-six-month programme rather than restating the v1 sub-programme risks.

R-A · Capital exhaustion before break-even
High

The fundamental programme-level risk: cumulative capital deployed exceeds available capital before customer revenue scales sufficiently to bridge the gap. Materialises if Series A closes late, or pilot conversion stalls, or hiring waterfall outpaces revenue ramp.

Mitigation: cash-flow schedule reviewed monthly against actual; hiring waterfall has 4-week deferral capacity in Phase 3; convertible-debt bridge from seed investors as backstop; capital plan sized at conservative-revenue scenario rather than base case.

R-B · Series A market window contraction
High

Indian deep-tech and cybersecurity venture market sentiment shifts during M14–M24 window; Series A round delayed beyond M24 target or completed at materially lower valuation, forcing additional dilution to raise the same capital.

Mitigation: opening institutional conversations early in Phase 3 rather than at Phase 3 close; building relationships with three lead-investor candidates rather than one; non-dilutive bridges (Startup India Seed, BIRAC instruments, RBI HaRBInger credibility); base-case revenue trajectory designed to support Series A even in tepid market.

R-C · Pilot conversion shortfall
High

The most consequential operational risk: fewer than three pilots convert to paid contracts by M22, undermining Series A defensibility and revenue trajectory.

Mitigation: signing four pilots (target three conversions) rather than three; standard MSA/DPA templates pre-cleared by external counsel to compress legal review cycle; named-engineer customer relationships built across Phase 2; white-glove customer success engagement; explicit pilot-to-paid commercial milestones embedded in pilot agreements.

R-D · Senior engineering attrition cascade
Medium

Loss of more than two senior engineers across the 36-month window — particularly the ML lead or Vertical Head — compromises engineering velocity, customer relationships, and tribal knowledge in ways that compound over subsequent quarters.

Mitigation: ESOP refresh tied to v1 GA milestone (cliff at M22) and to Series A close (cliff at M24); compensation bands reviewed annually against NCR market rates with above-band counter-offer authority pre-approved; cross-training matrix maintained; technical documentation gating in CI; externally-facing tech-blog programme builds individual reputation tying engineers to the programme.

R-E · Compliance audit findings cascade
Medium

SOC 2 Type II findings, ISO surveillance non-conformities, or pen-test critical findings that require sustained remediation effort, pulling engineering attention from product development and delaying GA.

Mitigation: pre-audit gap assessment by Big-4 firm in Phase 2; ISO 27001:2022 control framework substantially overlaps SOC 2 controls; backup audit firm under retainer; quarterly internal compliance reviews; remediation engineering staffed as a dedicated resource during Phase 3.

R-F · Customer-pod isolation incident
High

Cross-tenant data exposure due to misconfiguration or implementation defect in the pod-isolation architecture. Materialised consequence is existential to the LLP — recovery from such an event is structurally difficult in BFSI markets.

Mitigation: defence-in-depth across 6 control layers; isolation regression suite of 200+ test cases gating every release; external pen-test specifically targeting cross-tenant exposure; per-customer encryption keys ensuring storage-layer data is uninterpretable across tenant boundaries; cyber-liability insurance scaled to Rs 50 Cr coverage from Phase 3.

R-G · Hyperscaler competitive entry
Medium

AWS, Microsoft, Google, or an Indian hyperscaler launches a directly competitive Indian-sovereign BFSI-native offering at below-cost economics during the Phase 4 customer-acquisition window.

Mitigation: compliance depth and BFSI-native architecture are the structural moats — hyperscaler cost advantage cannot offset compliance disadvantage in regulated procurement; tier-1 reference customers establish switching cost; product roadmap maintains 6-month feature-velocity advantage; strategic-partnership conversations with hyperscalers as channel rather than direct competition.

Low

RBI, SEBI, or Data Protection Board issues clarifying circulars during the programme that materially change technical-control interpretation of existing master directions, requiring mid-programme architecture or compliance pivots.

Mitigation: external regulatory counsel under retainer with monthly briefing; ForgeVault compliance-mapping framework designed for incremental control updates; participation in RBI HaRBInger-track industry consultations for forward visibility; architecture decisions deliberately avoid contested control interpretations.

R-I · Cross-vertical contention at LLP level
Low

Capital, leadership attention, or shared-infrastructure capacity contention between cybersecurity, bioinformatics, and managed-services verticals at the LLP level, slowing cybersecurity-vertical execution.

Mitigation: each vertical operates as independent P&L subsidiary with its own engineering team and Vertical Head; shared infrastructure is capacity-allocated under documented schedule; LLP-level managing partner has explicit conflict-resolution authority; quarterly cross-vertical capacity review; cybersecurity vertical's customer-facing SLA commitments take precedence over internal cross-vertical compute contention.

Low

Geopolitical disruption, economic recession, banking-sector contraction, or other black-swan event that materially changes BFSI procurement budgets across the Phase 3 / Phase 4 window.

Mitigation: working-capital reserve absorbs 6-month cost-reduction scenario; programme has built-in cost-flexibility levers (hiring waterfall deferral, infrastructure expansion deferral, sales scale-out deferral) totaling approximately Rs 2 Cr of in-year cost reduction; conservative-revenue scenario already designed against subdued procurement environment.

LLP buyback / management buyout

If external capital markets unfavourable for Series B and operational economics support self-financed growth, LLP-level cash flows from cross-vertical operations buy back outside Series A holders at agreed valuation. Founder and management team retain full ownership. Slower growth trajectory but maximum control.

Owner: Board · Trigger: capital-market dependent

Operational sustainability metrics — tracked from M22

  • Net revenue retention (NRR). Target ≥ 115% — customer-base value grows year-over-year through expansion and upsell, even excluding new logos.

  • Magic number (revenue growth / sales spend). Target ≥ 0.8 — sales investment efficiently produces revenue growth at scale.
  • Rule of 40. Revenue growth percentage plus profit margin ≥ 40 — the SaaS-industry standard for healthy growth-vs-profitability balance.
